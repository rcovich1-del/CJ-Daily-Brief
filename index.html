07Strategy in Focus

Bull Put Spread — selected for a NORMAL regime (Tuesday)

A bull put spread sells a put and buys a further out-of-the-money put beneath it for a net credit. It profits from time decay and stable-to-rising prices, and its defined risk fits a calm, contango-vol tape where you want to be paid for a mildly bullish lean without unlimited exposure.

Worked example (delta / % terms): on an underlying near $100 with IV Rank ~90%, sell the ~25-delta put and buy a put ~5% lower, 30–45 days out. That collects roughly one-third of the spread width in credit (e.g. a $5-wide spread for ~$1.60). Max profit is the full credit if the stock finishes above the short strike; break-even is the short strike minus the credit; the position wins on time decay as long as price holds above the short put.

Main risk. Maximum loss is the spread width minus the credit (e.g. $5 − $1.60 = $3.40 per share), realized if the underlying closes below the long strike at expiry. Losses accrue faster than the credit as price falls through the short strike, and an overnight gap — an earnings miss or macro shock — can carry the position to full loss before you can adjust. A high IV Rank sweetens the premium but is itself the market's warning that larger moves are expected, so size every spread so a full-width loss is survivable and keep expiries clear of known earnings dates.
