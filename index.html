7Strategy in Focus — Bear Call Spread

Why this, now: the defense regime plus a Thursday rotation points to a bearish, defined-risk, premium-selling structure. With VIX in the moderate zone and the tape rejecting from the 20-/50-day, a bear call spread lets you lean short without unlimited risk and get paid by time decay and any IV normalization.

Educational example (delta / % terms): with an underlying trading at 100, sell the ~0.30-delta call roughly 3–5% above spot and buy the ~0.15-delta call further out-of-the-money, same 30–45-DTE expiration. A typical net credit runs ~25–35% of the strike width. Max profit = the credit, kept if the underlying finishes below the short strike. Breakeven = short strike + credit received. Max loss = width − credit.

Main risk: the loss is capped but real. The enemy is a sharp reversal — a relief bounce or short-squeeze after the AI-capex flush can drive the underlying up through both strikes for the full max loss, and a short call that goes in-the-money near expiry carries assignment/early-exercise (and ex-dividend) risk. Size small, define the exit before entry, and respect that this trade wants sideways-to-lower drift; a violent rally is exactly what it is not built for.
